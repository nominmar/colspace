Q1: Why do the same ratios of RGB values map to the same location on the CIE xy plane?

When you input RGB values, the transformation process normalizes them and converts them to the CIE xy chromaticity space. This space represents color independent of brightness, meaning the ratios between R, G, and B determine the chromaticity. For example:

  • RGB (10, 20, 30) (1:2:3 ratio) will map to the same xy location as RGB (50, 100, 150), since their relative proportions are identical.

This happens because the CIE xy plane focuses solely on chromaticity, and luminance (brightness) is handled separately by the Y component in the CIE XYZ space.

Q2: Will the location on the xy plane move in a straight line if I increment R while keeping G and B constant?

No, the movement on the xy plane will not be perfectly linear. This non-linearity arises from:

  • Gamma Correction: The RGB-to-XYZ transformation applies gamma correction, which is a non-linear adjustment of color values.
  • Normalization to Compute xy: The final xy coordinates are calculated as ratios of X, Y, and Z, which further introduces non-linearity.

As a result, while small increments in R may appear nearly linear, larger changes will follow a curved trajectory rather than a straight line.
